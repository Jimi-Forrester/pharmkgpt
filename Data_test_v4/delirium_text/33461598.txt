33461598|t|Metabotropic glutamate receptor 5 inhibits alpha-synuclein-induced microglia inflammation to protect from neurotoxicity in Parkinson's disease.
33461598|a|BACKGROUND: Microglia activation induced by alpha-synuclein (alpha-syn) is one of the most important factors in Parkinson's disease (PD) pathogenesis. However, the molecular mechanisms by which alpha-syn exerts neuroinflammation and neurotoxicity remain largely elusive. Targeting metabotropic glutamate receptor 5 (mGluR5) has been an attractive strategy to mediate microglia activation for neuroprotection, which might be an essential regulator to modulate alpha-syn-induced neuroinflammation for the treatment of PD. Here, we showed that mGluR5 inhibited alpha-syn-induced microglia inflammation to protect from neurotoxicity in vitro and in vivo. METHODS: Co-immunoprecipitation assays were utilized to detect the interaction between mGluR5 and alpha-syn in microglia. Griess, ELISA, real-time PCR, western blotting, and immunofluorescence assays were used to detect the regulation of alpha-syn-induced inflammatory signaling, cytokine secretion, and lysosome-dependent degradation. RESULTS: alpha-syn selectively interacted with mGluR5 but not mGluR3, and alpha-syn N terminal deletion region was essential for binding to mGluR5 in co-transfected HEK293T cells. The interaction between these two proteins was further detected in BV2 microglia, which was inhibited by the mGluR5 specific agonist CHPG without effect by its selective antagonist MTEP. Moreover, in both BV2 cells and primary microglia, activation of mGluR5 by CHPG partially inhibited alpha-syn-induced inflammatory signaling and cytokine secretion and also inhibited the microglia activation to protect from neurotoxicity. We further found that alpha-syn overexpression decreased mGluR5 expression via a lysosomal pathway, as evidenced by the lysosomal inhibitor, NH4Cl, by blocking mGluR5 degradation, which was not evident with the proteasome inhibitor, MG132. Additionally, co-localization of mGluR5 with alpha-syn was detected in lysosomes as merging with its marker, LAMP-1. Consistently, in vivo experiments with LPS- or AAV-alpha-syn-induced rat PD model also confirmed that alpha-syn accelerated lysosome-dependent degradation of mGluR5 involving a complex, to regulate neuroinflammation. Importantly, the binding is strengthened with LPS or alpha-syn overexpression but alleviated by urate, a potential clinical biomarker for PD. CONCLUSIONS: These findings provided evidence for a novel mechanism by which the association of alpha-syn with mGluR5 was attributed to alpha-syn-induced microglia activation via modulation of mGluR5 degradation and its intracellular signaling. This may be a new molecular target for an effective therapeutic strategy for PD pathology.
