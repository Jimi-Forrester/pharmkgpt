38308496|t|Synaptic adhesion molecule protocadherin-gammaC5 mediates beta-amyloid-induced neuronal hyperactivity and cognitive deficits in Alzheimer's disease.
38308496|a|Neuronal hyperactivity induced by beta-amyloid (Abeta) is an early pathological feature in Alzheimer's disease (AD) and contributes to cognitive decline in AD progression. However, the underlying mechanisms are still unclear. Here, we revealed that Abeta increased the expression level of synaptic adhesion molecule protocadherin-gammaC5 (Pcdh-gammaC5) in a Ca2+ -dependent manner, associated with aberrant elevation of synapses in both Abeta-treated neurons in vitro and the cortex of APP/PS1 mice in vivo. By using Pcdhgc5 gene knockout mice, we demonstrated the critical function of Pcdh-gammaC5 in regulating neuronal synapse formation, synaptic transmission, and cognition. To further investigate the role of Pcdh-gammaC5 in AD pathogenesis, the aberrantly enhanced expression of Pcdh-gammaC5 in the brain of APP/PS1 mice was knocked down by shRNA. Downregulation of Pcdh-gammaC5 efficiently rescued neuronal hyperactivity and impaired cognition in APP/PS1 mice. Our findings revealed the pathophysiological role of Pcdh-gammaC5 in mediating Abeta-induced neuronal hyperactivity and cognitive deficits in AD and identified a novel mechanism underlying AD pathogenesis.
