37770136|t|Myricetin causes site-specific DNA damage via reactive oxygen species generation by redox interactions with copper ions.
37770136|a|Myricetin (MYR), found in tea and berries, may have preventive effects on diseases, including Alzheimer's disease and cancer. However, MYR is also a mutagen, inducing DNA damage in the presence of metal ions. We have studied the molecular mechanisms of DNA damage by MYR in the presence of Cu(II) (MYR+Cu). Using 32P-5'-end-labeled DNA fragments, we analyzed site-specific DNA damage caused by MYR+Cu. MYR+Cu caused concentration-dependent DNA strand breaks and base alterations, leading to cleavage of DNA at thymine, cytosine, and guanine nucleotides. Formation of the oxidative DNA damage indicator, 8-oxo-7,8-dihydro-2'-deoxyguanosine (8-oxodG), in calf thymus DNA was increased by MYR+Cu. The production of 8-oxodG in MYR-treated HL-60 cells was significantly higher than in HP100 cells, which are more resistant to H2O2 than are HL-60 cells. Reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavengers were used to elucidate the mechanism of DNA damage. DNA damage was not inhibited by typical free hydroxyl radical ( OH) scavengers such as ethanol, mannitol, or sodium formate. However, methional, catalase, and bathocuproine inhibited DNA damage induced by MYR+Cu. These results suggest that H2O2, Cu(I), and ROS other than  OH are involved in MYR+Cu-induced DNA damage. We conclude that the Cu(I)/Cu(II) redox cycle and concomitant H2O2 production via autoxidation of MYR generate a complex of H2O2 and Cu(I), probably Cu(I)-hydroperoxide, which induces oxidative DNA damage.
