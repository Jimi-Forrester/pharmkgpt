31143694|t|The neuroprotective mechanism of cinnamaldehyde against amyloid-beta in neuronal SHSY5Y cell line: The role of N-methyl-D-aspartate, ryanodine, and adenosine receptors and glycogen synthase kinase-3beta.
31143694|a|OBJECTIVE: Cinnamaldehyde may be responsible for some health benefits of cinnamon such as its neuroprotective effects. We aimed to investigate the cinnamaldehyde neuroprotective effects against amyloid beta (Abeta) in neuronal SHSY5Y cells and evaluate the contribution of N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA), ryanodine, and adenosine receptors and glycogen synthase kinase (GSK)-3beta, to its neuroprotective effects. MATERIALS AND METHODS: After seeding the cells in 96-well plates, adenosine (20, 40, 80, and 120 microM), NMDA (20, 40, 80, and 120 microM), and dantrolene (as a ryanodine receptor antagonist; 2, 4, 6, 8, and 16 microM) were added to the medium containing Abeta25-35 and/or cinnamaldehyde. The 3-[4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl]-2,5-diphenyl tetrazolium bromide method was used to assess neurotoxicity and western blot to measure the GSK-3beta protein level. RESULTS: Cinnamaldehyde (15, 20, 23, and 25 muM) significantly reversed Abeta-induced toxicity in SHSY5Y neuronal cells. Adenosine (20, 40, 80 and 120 muM) inhibited the neuroprotective effects of cinnamaldehyde (15 muM). NMDA (20, 40, 80, and 120 muM) reduced cinnamaldehyde (15 and 23 muM) neuroprotective effects against Abeta neurotoxicity. Dantrolene (2, 4, 8, and 16 muM) significantly reduced cinnamaldehyde (15 muM) neuroprotective effects. Cinnamaldehyde (15 and 23 muM) suppressed the Abeta-induced increment of GSK-3beta protein level. CONCLUSION: NMDA and adenosine receptors suppression together with ryanodine receptors stimulation may be relevant to cinnamaldehyde neuroprotective effects against Abeta neurotoxicity. Moreover, the inhibition of GSK-3beta may contribute to the cinnamaldehyde neuroprotection.
