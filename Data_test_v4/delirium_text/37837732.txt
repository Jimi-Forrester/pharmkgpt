37837732|t|Protein retention in the endoplasmic reticulum rescues Abeta toxicity in Drosophila.
37837732|a|Amyloid beta (Abeta) accumulation is a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease. In adult Drosophila brains, human Abeta overexpression harms climbing and lifespan. It's uncertain whether Abeta is intrinsically toxic or activates downstream neurodegeneration pathways. Our study uncovers a novel protective role against Abeta toxicity: intra-endoplasmic reticulum (ER) protein accumulation with a focus on laminin and collagen subunits. Despite high Abeta, laminin B1 (LanB1) overexpression robustly counters toxicity, suggesting a potential Abeta resistance mechanism. Other laminin subunits and collagen IV also alleviate Abeta toxicity; combining them with LanB1 augments the effect. Imaging reveals ER retention of LanB1 without altering Abeta secretion. LanB1's rescue function operates independently of the IRE1alpha/XBP1 ER stress response. ER-targeted GFP overexpression also mitigates Abeta toxicity, highlighting broader ER protein retention advantages. Proof-of-principle tests in murine hippocampal slices using mouse Lamb1 demonstrate ER retention in transduced cells, indicating a conserved mechanism. Though ER protein retention generally harms, it could paradoxically counter neuronal Abeta toxicity, offering a new therapeutic avenue for Alzheimer's disease.
